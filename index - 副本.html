The serial killer known as 'the Wasatch Butcher' is still at large.

By Rowan Sullivan r.sullivan@mycityjournals.com
We're sorry, but we don't actually have any leads on any suspects at the moment, so we can't create a portrait.

Police sketch of the suspect, last seen near Corner Canyon.

As the investigation into the "Wasatch Butcher" enters its fourth month, the Draper Police Department confirms the case remains in a state of "high-level evidentiary synthesis." Despite thousands of man-hours, authorities are still navigating a complex data-processing phase.

Police Chief Francesca Nicole Lee stated that the current effort is focused on "multidimensional resource integration." According to Chief Lee, the department is executing rigorous procedural protocols to categorize unstructured information gathered over the last ninety days.

Due to a lack of distinct forensic signatures, the suspect has demonstrated what Chief Lee calls "socio-environmental stealth." "We are monitoring a shadow with no defined perimeter," she added. "Our current posture is one of total community-wide collaborative defense."

A new "Community Safety Liaison Committee" has been formed to streamline communication, though the department faces a significant backlog of unverified tips. Technical teams are currently implementing computer-aided systems to manage physical surveillance logs.

"Every locked door remains a primary line of defense," Chief Lee concluded. As of press time, no specific targets of interest have been identified. Residents are once again urged to follow the principle of "If you see something, say something."

A Match Made in the Valley

GUILTY

Diane, June 2015

By James P. "Sulley" Sullivan | Special Features Correspondent

In the sun-drenched corners of our quiet Draper suburb, there exists a home that seems to have stepped right out of a vintage postcard. It is the residence of Rowan and Diane Sullivan—a couple that many in our community have come to define as the very blueprint of "Salt of the Earth."

Rowan Sullivan, 28, is a face many of you will recognize from right here at the Draper Journals. As one of our most dedicated investigative reporters, Rowan brings that classic Irish work ethic and a "good lad" charm to every story he touches. Born into a bustling, large family, he carries the warmth of a man who knows the value of community and the weight of an honest word. With his signature black hair and those striking blue eyes that neighbors describe as "consistently kind," Rowan is often seen tipping his hat to passersby, embodying the reliable spirit of a true Draper gentleman.

But every great storyteller needs a muse, and Rowan found his in Diane. At 29, Diane Sullivan is the golden-haired heart of their household. Originally hailing from the southern reaches of Hildale, Utah, Diane has woven a new life here that is nothing short of artistic. While she gracefully identifies as a "simple housewife," there is nothing simple about the way she orchestrates her home. From the pristine symmetry of her garden to the legendary perfection of her domestic craft, Diane elevates housewifery to a form of high art.

Neighbors often speak of the "serene aura" that surrounds the Sullivan home. Whether she is captured in a moment of quiet reflection at her easel—being a gifted amateur painter—or seen in her signature shoulder-length blonde and traditional prairie dresses, Diane exudes a timeless virtue. Some in the younger crowd might even recognize her soothing, melodic voice from her amateur creative audio projects online, where she shares the tranquility of her home life with the world.

"They just fit," says one local grocer. "Rowan has that honest, steady energy, and Diane has this way of making everything around her look... perfect. It’s like they’ve created their own little world where nothing ever goes wrong."

As they celebrate another year of marital bliss, the Sullivans remain a testament to the fact that in a world of constant change, some things—like a well-tended hearth and a shared look of devotion—remain sacred. They are more than just neighbors; they are the quiet, perfect pulse of Draper.

FRIDAY READER | June 15, 2015

The Sullivans' Friday Stories

This is indeed a happy and heartwarming story. Indeed.

By Rowan Sullivan & Diane Sullivan

It started with a simple question. Where do the missing go? In a town like Draper, people don't just vanish. They leave traces. A receipt at the gas station. A neighbor's wave. A footprint in the snow. But for the last three months, the Wasatch Butcher has been erasing people as if they were never there.

I have spent my career chasing stories. I have looked into the eyes of grieving mothers and corrupt politicians. But I have never looked as closely as I have at my own reflection. Or at Diane's, as she stands by the sink, washing the red clay from her hands after a long evening in the garden.

"The roses are hungry this year, Rowan," she told me last night. Her voice was soft, like the rustle of dry leaves. We were eating dinner—steak, rare, just how she likes it. The knife in her hand was steady. It was the same steadiness I saw when we met twenty years ago.

We are a partnership. That is what marriage is, isn't it? A shared burden. A shared secret. When I write about the Butcher, I am not just reporting. I am curating. I am crafting the narrative. And Diane? She is the artist. The world is her canvas, and she paints in broad, unseen strokes.

Some say the Butcher is a monster. A beast hiding in the canyons. They are wrong. The Butcher is your neighbor. The Butcher is the person who brings you a casserole when you are sick. The Butcher is the one who waves at you every morning as you drive to work.

We often joke about it, Diane and I. "Did you see the paper today?" she'll ask, wiping a speck of dirt from her cheek. "They think he's six feet tall." And we will laugh. Because the truth is so much simpler. The truth is sitting right there at the breakfast table.

So, dear reader, as you finish your coffee and prepare for your Friday, ask yourself: Do you really know who lives next door? Do you know what lies beneath their garden? Or are you, like everyone else, just happy to admire the roses?
